The Case: Clopidogrel vs. Ticagrelor (Antiplatelet Therapy, Post-ACS)
Both drugs do the same job: P2Y12 receptor inhibition for antiplatelet therapy after acute coronary syndrome. But on a Medicare Advantage formulary, they sit in wildly different pricing tiers, and only one of them is vulnerable to phenoconversion failure.
Pricing differential (CMS formulary data, 2026):
Generic clopidogrel runs as low as $3 per fill with coupons, with an average retail price around $100.  On most Medicare Part D formularies, it sits at Tier 1 (preferred generic), which means $0 copay at preferred pharmacies or under $5 at standard pharmacies. 
Generic ticagrelor has an average retail price of about $422, though coupons can bring it to around $28.  With commercial insurance, generic ticagrelor is typically Tier 1 or Tier 2, with copays ranging from $5 to $30.  Brand Brilinta runs $450 to $665 per month cash, and on Medicare Part D it typically sits at Tier 3 with copays potentially reaching $47 or more, and patients in the coverage gap face significant out-of-pocket costs. 
So the price gap: $0-5/month for clopidogrel vs. $10-50/month for generic ticagrelor, or $47-95+/month for brand Brilinta. A plan would default to clopidogrel as the preferred low-cost antiplatelet every time.
Why this is a phenoconversion disaster:
Clopidogrel is a prodrug that requires CYP2C19 activation to work. No CYP2C19 activation = no active metabolite = no platelet inhibition = the drug is a sugar pill. Here's where your phenoconversion engine lights up:
If the same discharge list includes omeprazole (a weak CYP2C19 inhibitor, prescribed for GI protection in nearly every post-ACS patient), your engine computes: CYP2C19 baseline activity multiplied by 0.63 (the omeprazole multiplier from your spec). For a CYP2C19 *1/*2 intermediate metabolizer with baseline activity 1.0, the effective activity drops to 0.63, pushing them into functionally poor metabolizer territory. Clopidogrel activation is now clinically insufficient.
If the list includes fluvoxamine (strong CYP2C19 inhibitor, sometimes co-prescribed for comorbid depression/anxiety), the multiplier is 0.15. The patient is now a functional CYP2C19 ultra-poor metabolizer regardless of genotype. Clopidogrel is completely dead.
Ticagrelor is CYP2C19-independent. It's a direct-acting P2Y12 inhibitor, not a prodrug. Its antiplatelet effect is not affected by CYP2C19 genotype or phenoconversion status. Ticagrelor has consistent efficacy regardless of CYP2C19 status. 
The side effect module angle:
Your side effect severity module adds a third dimension. Ticagrelor carries a boxed warning about the risks of premature discontinuation, including increased rates of MI, stroke, and death.  Ticagrelor is more effective than clopidogrel based on clinical trial data.  But ticagrelor has its own side effect burden: dyspnea (a very common complaint, ~14%), increased bleeding risk, and bradyarrhythmias. Your WSI module would compute the severity comparison and potentially show ticagrelor as EQUIVALENT or WORSE on intrinsic side effect burden despite being pharmacogenomically safer for this patient.
